Learning Styles & Approaches

Montessori? Play-based? Forest School? Every approach is really an answer to one question: how do young children learn best? Here's each philosophy in plain language — what it believes, what the classroom looks like — plus a ready-made shopping bundle to stock it. Most great preschools blend two or three; there is no wrong door.

Step 1

Choose an approach

Play-Based Learning

"Play is the work of childhood."

The nature of this approach

Play-based classrooms trust that children learn academic and social skills best through play, not in spite of it. Teachers set up rich centers — dramatic play, blocks, sensory, art — and then join, extend, and gently steer the play toward language, math, and cooperation goals. It looks like fun; underneath it is careful design.

What the classroom looks like: open centers, long uninterrupted play blocks, teachers on the floor asking "what happens if...?", learning goals hidden inside pretend restaurants and block cities.

Who it suits: nearly everyone — it's the backbone of most American preschools and pairs beautifully with every other approach on this page.

Stock the play-based classroom

  • Loose parts play kit
  • Play silks
  • Pretend play sets
  • Sand & water table
  • Building blocks
  • Dress-up trunk
  • Teacher read: Purposeful Play
  • Mrs. Bear's pick: It's Ok to Play With Friends
Mrs. Bear's tip: the secret of play-based teaching is the question, not the toy. "Tell me about your building" grows more language than any flashcard ever printed.

Montessori

"Help me do it myself."

The nature of this approach

Born from Dr. Maria Montessori's work in 1907, this approach believes children are naturally driven to learn and gain deep confidence from real independence. Children choose their own "work" from beautiful, self-correcting materials on low shelves, and practical life — pouring, buttoning, food prep — is honored as real curriculum.

What the classroom looks like: calm and orderly; natural wood everywhere; child-sized real tools; children working alone or in pairs on floor mats; the teacher ("guide") observing and giving quiet one-on-one lessons.

Who it suits: children who thrive on order and independence; programs that value concentration and self-direction over group instruction.

Stock the Montessori shelf

  • Practical life pouring set
  • Knobbed cylinders
  • Sandpaper letters
  • Dressing frames (buttons, zippers)
  • Child-size cleaning set
  • Low open shelving
  • Work rugs
  • Teacher read: The Montessori Toddler
Mrs. Bear's tip: you don't have to be a certified Montessori school to borrow the best part — put real, breakable, child-sized tools in children's hands and watch how carefully they rise to it.

Reggio Emilia

"The child has a hundred languages."

The nature of this approach

From the town of Reggio Emilia, Italy, this approach sees children as capable researchers who express ideas in a "hundred languages" — paint, clay, light, movement, conversation. Curriculum emerges from the children's own questions and grows into long-running projects, which teachers document with photos and quotes displayed at child height.

What the classroom looks like: an art studio feel ("atelier"); light tables and mirrors; children's work and words on every wall; natural and found materials; projects that last weeks because the children keep asking questions.

Who it suits: programs with creative, observant teachers; families who love seeing thinking made visible.

Stock the atelier

  • Light table
  • Translucent manipulatives
  • Child-safe mirrors
  • Liquid watercolors
  • Clay (air-dry, classroom pack)
  • Natural loose parts
  • Photo printer (documentation)
  • Teacher read: Working in the Reggio Way
Mrs. Bear's tip: start Reggio-style with one wall. Photograph one project, print the children's exact words underneath, hang it at their eye level — and watch parents stop and read every word at pickup.

Waldorf

"Rhythm, reverence, and imagination first."

The nature of this approach

Founded by Rudolf Steiner in 1919, Waldorf early childhood protects imagination fiercely. Days follow a steady, comforting rhythm — the same songs, the same baking day, the same seasonal festivals — and toys are simple and natural on purpose: a knot of silk can be a baby, a cape, or the sea. Academics wait; wonder comes first.

What the classroom looks like: soft colors, wool and wood, a nature table that changes with the seasons, teachers singing transitions instead of announcing them, real bread rising somewhere.

Who it suits: programs that want calm, screen-free, deeply rhythmic days; families drawn to simplicity and seasonal celebration.

Stock the Waldorf classroom

  • Play silks (seasonal colors)
  • Wooden peg dolls
  • Beeswax crayons
  • Wool felt
  • Wooden rainbow stacker
  • Playstands
  • Nature table supplies
  • Teacher read: Heaven on Earth
Mrs. Bear's tip: steal Waldorf's superpower — sing your transitions. A clean-up song ends the negotiation before it starts, and even your wiggliest friend can't argue with a melody.

Forest School (Nature-Based)

"There's no bad weather, only bad clothing."

The nature of this approach

The outdoor one — and it's exactly what it sounds like. Forest School moves learning outside for long, regular stretches in all weather, trusting nature as the richest classroom there is. Children climb, dig, balance, and take sensible risks with support, building resilience, focus, and strong bodies along with their science vocabulary.

What the classroom looks like: mud kitchens in full service, magnifier in every pocket, rain suits on hooks, a teacher asking "how could we find out?" next to a puddle instead of mopping it up.

Who it suits: programs with any usable outdoor space (a courtyard counts!); children who learn best in motion — which is most of them.

Stock the outdoor classroom

  • Mud kitchen
  • Rain suits / waterproof coveralls
  • Magnifying glasses (class set)
  • Bug catchers
  • Child-size garden tools
  • Outdoor balance equipment
  • Outdoor first aid kit
  • Teacher read: Balanced and Barefoot
Mrs. Bear's tip: in Florida, our "forest" is shade sails and a rain plan — nature-based works anywhere. Start with one all-weather outdoor hour a day and watch the afternoon behavior chart improve by itself.

HighScope

"Plan, do, review."

The nature of this approach

Built on decades of research (including the famous Perry Preschool Study), HighScope centers on active participatory learning and a daily rhythm called plan-do-review: children say what they intend to do, go do it, then come back and reflect on how it went. That tiny loop builds executive function — planning, follow-through, self-evaluation — the skills that predict school success.

What the classroom looks like: clearly labeled interest areas, a consistent daily routine posted in pictures, small-group times, teachers recording what children actually do and building tomorrow's plan from it.

Who it suits: programs that love structure with freedom inside it; teams that want research-backed routines and strong assessment habits.

Stock the HighScope classroom

  • Picture labels for every area
  • Clear labeled storage bins
  • Visual daily routine cards
  • Pocket chart (planning time)
  • Sand timers
  • Counting manipulatives
  • Observation note supplies
  • Teacher read: Educating Young Children
Mrs. Bear's tip: plan-do-review works at any preschool: at morning circle, ask each child "what will you do at centers?" — then at closing circle, "how did it go?" Two questions, zero cost, executive function every single day.
